On Mute.

Not on Pause.

How a 50-word calendar mention became a 500-word editorial on body language in the virtual workplace — and why it became the highest-engaging content of the quarter.

By Julie Allen Cisco Employee Connection Published 2020
The muted-but-visible dynamic of pandemic communication.

You may be on mute, but your body language isn't. In 2020, this simple observation became the foundation for reimagining how we think about virtual presence, turning a routine calendar announcement into essential reading for 75,000 employees navigating the sudden shift to remote work.

The Brief That Almost Wasn't

The request was straightforward: write a "Mark Your Calendar" mention for an upcoming CEC event about communication during the pandemic. Fifty words, maximum. Standard corporate communications fare — the kind of notice that typically achieves a 2% click-through rate on a good day.

But this wasn't a standard moment. It was April 2020. Cisco employees had been working from kitchen tables and bedroom corners for weeks. Everyone was exhausted from trying to recreate office dynamics through screens. The last thing anyone needed was another calendar reminder.

"Calendar mentions are the wallpaper of corporate comms. Nobody reads them. This moment deserved more than that."

The Universal Truth Nobody Was Discussing

Video calls had exposed something fascinating: we were all amateur broadcasters now, performing for an audience we couldn't fully see. Half the team in boxes on screen, the other half just black rectangles with names. Everyone muted, everyone visible, everyone struggling to read the room when the room didn't exist.

The real story wasn't about technology or tips for better lighting. It was about the human challenge of maintaining connection when our primary communication tool — body language — had been compressed into a 2-inch square.

What the Editorial Explored

The Mute Button Paradox

You're silenced but still performing. Every eye roll, every slump, every distracted phone check — all broadcast in HD while you think you're invisible.

Reading the Unreadable Room

How do you gauge engagement when half your audience is off-camera? The article explored new cues: chat activity, response time, the energy in voices.

The Gallery View Dilemma

Seeing yourself constantly while trying to focus on others. The cognitive load of being simultaneously presenter and audience.

Digital Eye Contact

Looking at the camera feels weird. Looking at faces feels natural. But only one creates actual eye contact for viewers.

From Calendar Mention to Must-Read

Instead of fifty words about an event, I wrote five hundred words about an experience everyone was having. The editorial acknowledged what we were all feeling but hadn't articulated: that professional communication had fundamentally changed overnight, and we were all figuring it out together.

  • Make eye contact across the etherverse Train yourself to look at the camera, not the screen. It feels unnatural but creates connection for everyone watching you.
  • Your mute button isn't an invisibility cloak People can see you even when they can't hear you. Maintain engagement as if you were unmuted.
  • Read the new room signals Watch chat engagement, listen for verbal cues, pay attention to who's camera goes off (they might be struggling).
  • Exaggerate to communicate Subtle nods don't translate. Bigger gestures, clearer facial expressions, more deliberate reactions.
  • Create presence through participation Use reactions, chat, and verbal acknowledgments to signal engagement when your video is off.

When Editorial Meets Moment

The piece resonated because it wasn't about the event — it was about the shared experience of trying to maintain human connection through technology. It gave language to frustrations people couldn't quite name and validation to struggles they thought were unique to them.

Teams started sharing it in their meetings as a conversation starter. Managers used it to open discussions about communication norms. The event it was supposed to promote? Attendance exceeded targets by 300%.

Why This Matters Beyond the Pandemic

The success of "On Mute. Not on Pause." proved something important about internal communications: employees don't just want information, they want insight. They don't just need announcements, they need acknowledgment of their experience.

"A calendar mention can be editorial if you treat your audience like readers."

Three years later, hybrid work is permanent and the lessons from this piece still apply. Virtual presence is a skill. Digital body language is a language. And sometimes the best corporate communication is the kind that admits we're all just figuring it out together.

The Editorial Approach

This piece set a precedent at Cisco: any communication can be elevated if you respect your audience enough to give them something worth reading. It's not about making everything longer — it's about making everything matter.

The editorial proved that even in corporate communications, especially in corporate communications, there's room for the kind of writing that makes people stop scrolling and start thinking. Sometimes that's fifty words. Sometimes it's five hundred. The skill is knowing which moment demands which.
