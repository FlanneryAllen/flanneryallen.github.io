You May Be on Mute,

But Your Body Language Isn't

By Julie Allen April 2020

We're all living in boxes now. Some of us in high-definition, some pixelated, some just black rectangles with our names. And while you may be on mute during that morning standup, your body language is broadcasting loud and clear.

Four weeks into remote work, and we've become amateur broadcasters, performing for audiences we can't fully see. That slight eye roll when someone extends the meeting? Everyone saw it. The moment you picked up your phone? Yep, caught that too. The slump that happens forty minutes in? It's all there, captured in pixels and transmitted across the etherverse.

The challenge isn't technology — we've figured out how to unmute (mostly). It's that our primary communication tool, body language, has been compressed into a two-inch square. We're trying to read rooms that don't exist, gauge energy we can't feel, and maintain connections through screens that only show us what fits in frame.

Reading the Unreadable Room

In the office, you knew if your joke landed. You could feel when attention drifted. A glance around the conference table told you everything. Now? Half your audience is off-camera, a quarter are muted, and you're not sure if that silence means deep thought or if everyone's on email.

The cues haven't disappeared — they've just changed. Watch the chat. It's the new side conversation, the digital equivalent of whispered comments. Response time tells you engagement levels. The energy in voices when people do unmute? That's your applause meter. And when cameras start going off one by one? You've lost the room.

The Gallery View Dilemma

Here's what nobody talks about: seeing yourself constantly while trying to focus on others is exhausting. You're simultaneously presenter and audience, performer and critic. That cognitive load? It's real. You're managing your appearance, your background, your reactions, all while trying to actually listen and contribute.

And then there's the eye contact problem. Looking at faces on screen feels natural. Looking at the camera feels like staring into the void. But only one creates actual eye contact for your viewers. We're all learning to perform for the green dot, not the human faces below it.

Creating Digital Presence

  • Your mute button isn't an invisibility cloak You're still on stage, even when silenced. Maintain engagement as if everyone can hear your thoughts — because they can see them on your face.
  • Exaggerate to communicate Subtle nods don't translate through pixels. Bigger gestures, clearer facial expressions, more deliberate reactions. Think stage acting, not film.
  • Make eye contact with the void Train yourself to look at the camera when speaking. It feels unnatural but creates connection. Your audience sees you looking at them, not at yourself.
  • Use everything available Reactions, chat, verbal acknowledgments when off-camera. Presence isn't just visual anymore — it's participation across all channels.
  • Reset your energy Stand up between meetings. Move around when off-camera. Physical energy translates to digital presence more than you think.

The Human Truth

Here's what we're all discovering: authentic connection doesn't require physical proximity, but it does require intentionality. We can't rely on the casual collision of office life anymore — the coffee run conversations, the hallway check-ins, the energy you absorb just by being in the same space.

Instead, we're learning to be deliberately present. To unmute for the laugh. To turn on video for the important moments. To type the encouragement in chat that we might have conveyed with a smile. We're translating human connection into digital signals, one interaction at a time.

"We're not just working from home. We're learning a new language — one where presence is performed, connection is crafted, and a two-inch square becomes our stage."

The Path Forward

This isn't temporary. Even when we return to offices, these skills — digital presence, intentional connection, reading virtual rooms — will remain essential. We've been forced to become fluent in digital body language, and there's no going back to monolingual communication.

So yes, you may be on mute. But you're not on pause. You're broadcasting, connecting, and communicating in ways we're all still learning to decode. The grammar of virtual presence is still being written, and we're all co-authors, one awkward unmute at a time.

Remember: everyone else is struggling with this too. That frozen smile when the connection lags? We've all been there. The panic when you realize you've been talking on mute? Universal experience. The exhaustion of performing presence all day? Shared by everyone in every box on your screen.

We're all figuring this out together, one pixel at a time.
