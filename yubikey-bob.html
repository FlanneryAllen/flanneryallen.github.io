This is Bob.

Be Like Bob. Get a YubiKey.

How a stick figure in a Cisco hat became the most successful security adoption campaign in company history — and sold out global YubiKey inventory in the process.

By Julie Allen Cisco Employee Connection Published July 2019

Meet Bob. He Just Got a YubiKey.

Now he logs in 4 times faster.

The Challenge

The Ask
Get Cisco employees to adopt YubiKey hardware security keys as backup authentication
The Problem
Nobody reads security mandates, especially ones requiring hardware orders
The Audience
75,000+ employees across 190 countries, all suffering from security fatigue

Security communications at Cisco had a problem. Years of mandatory training, phishing simulations, and policy updates had created an audience that reflexively ignored anything security-related. The IT Security team needed employees to voluntarily order and configure YubiKeys — physical hardware tokens for two-factor authentication. Traditional approaches weren't working.

The brief was straightforward: increase YubiKey adoption. But getting people to take action on yet another security requirement? That required rethinking everything about how we communicated security at Cisco.

The Insight

"What if we stopped talking about security and started talking about convenience?"

The YubiKey wasn't just about security — it was actually faster than typing codes from your phone. Four times faster, in fact. But nobody knew that because every communication led with compliance and risk. We needed to flip the script.

Enter Bob: a deliberately simple stick figure wearing a Cisco hat. Not a stock photo employee. Not a technical diagram. Just Bob. The format borrowed from the internet's "This is Bob. Be like Bob" meme — instantly recognizable, shareable, and most importantly, not what anyone expected from IT Security.

The Approach

  • Lead with benefit, not compliance: "Securely log in 4 times faster" became our headline, not "New security requirement."
  • Humanize through humor: Bob loses his phone. Bob forgets his charger. Bob is every one of us. The problems were relatable, not technical.
  • Simplify ruthlessly: No jargon. No acronyms. Just "This is Bob. Bob got a YubiKey. Now Bob logs in faster. Be like Bob."
  • Make it visual: The stick figure format meant people could understand the entire message in seconds, even in a busy Slack channel or email preview.
  • Create social proof: "Be like Bob" wasn't a command from IT — it was peer influence. Bob was your colleague who figured out the life hack.

The Execution

The campaign rolled out across Cisco Employee Connection (CEC), our internal communications platform, in July 2019. But Bob didn't stay there. Within hours, employees were sharing Bob in Slack channels, WebEx spaces, and email threads. Bob became a verb — "Have you Bobbed yet?" Security leaders started using Bob slides in their presentations. Regional offices translated Bob into local languages.

What started as a single piece of content became Cisco's unofficial mascot for "security done right." Bob appeared on office posters, in team meetings, even on some custom t-shirts that employees made themselves. The IT Security team, initially skeptical of the informal approach, became Bob's biggest champions when they saw the metrics.

Why Bob Worked

Bob succeeded because he didn't feel like corporate communications. In a landscape of formal security mandates and technical documentation, Bob was refreshingly human. He proved that even in a technical company like Cisco, the best way to drive behavior change isn't through more information — it's through better storytelling.

The Long-Term Impact

Bob didn't just sell YubiKeys. He changed how Cisco approaches change management. The campaign became required reading in the company's communication guidelines. IT teams started requesting "their own Bob" for everything from software migrations to policy updates.

Three years later, "Bob" is still shorthand at Cisco for communication that actually works. New employees hear about Bob in onboarding. Security teams around the world have created their own versions. One simple stick figure proved that the best security tool isn't always technical — sometimes it's just good storytelling.

"Bob proved that employees don't resist change. They resist boring communications about change."

Lessons Learned

  • Humor disarms resistance: People share what makes them smile. Bob made security shareable.
  • Simple beats sophisticated: A stick figure outperformed every glossy campaign that year.
  • Benefits beat features: "4 times faster" mattered more than any technical specification.
  • Memes are a language: Borrowing internet culture made corporate comms feel peer-to-peer.
  • Metrics don't lie: Bob's success gave permission for more creative approaches company-wide.
